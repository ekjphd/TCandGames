This document explains the three major assignments in detail. Each is shared in a discussion forum and responded to by peers using the modified Critical Response Process described in the syllabus. The assignments build on one another: the first sharpens observation, the second applies a research method, and the third extends analysis into an original contribution.

## Major Assignment 1: Principle in the Wild

**Due: Week 5 (Sunday, September 27)**

### Assignment rationale

The hardest part of analysis is learning to see. Before you can argue that a system succeeds or fails, you have to be able to name precisely what it is doing. This assignment asks you to do exactly one thing well: locate a single technical communication principle operating in a real, nontraditional site, and show it in action. You are not yet evaluating whether the design is good. You are training your eye to recognize a principle when it appears in the wild, outside the conventional manual or document where technical communication is usually studied. This skill underwrites everything else you will do this term.

### Where to start

Choose one principle from the course so far (Chapters 1 through 3 cover Principles 1 through 12). Then find a nontraditional site where that principle is clearly operating: a video game, an app, a kiosk, a wearable, a vehicle interface, an appliance, or any interactive system that is not a traditional document. Pick a site you can observe closely and repeatedly, either by using it yourself or by studying no-commentary gameplay or interface footage. Once you have a principle and a site, identify the specific moment or feature where the principle is doing its work, and plan how you will show it (not just tell about it).

### What is due

A short multimodal analysis that demonstrates the principle in action, posted to the discussion forum. The deliverable may take one of these forms:

- A narrated screen capture (2 to 4 minutes) walking through the moment where the principle operates.
- An annotated image set (4 to 6 images) with captions identifying the principle at each point.
- An illustrated written analysis (roughly 600 to 900 words) with embedded screenshots.

Whatever the form, your submission must name the principle, point to the specific feature or moment, and explain how the principle operates there. Include two or three specific questions you want peer feedback on, and indicate which areas you are open to receiving opinions on (per the Critical Response Process).

### Tips and resources

- Narrow ruthlessly. One principle, one clear moment, is far stronger than a tour of many.
- Show, do not summarize. The point is to make the principle visible to your reader, not to describe it abstractly.
- Resist evaluating. If you find yourself arguing the design is effective or flawed, save that for Assignment 2. Here, just demonstrate that the principle is present.
- Choose a site outside the AAA, "best games" mainstream where you can. Indie games, mobile apps, older software, and physical interfaces often show principles more clearly and counter the scope limits of the field.
- Review the relevant chapter and Appendix B (the principle definitions) before you start.

## Major Assignment 2: Heuristic Evaluation or Usability Study

**Due: Week 10 (Sunday, November 1)**

### Assignment rationale

Practitioners do not evaluate interfaces by intuition. They use structured, recognized methods that produce evidence others can scrutinize and act on. This assignment moves you from noticing a principle to systematically evaluating a system against a defined set of criteria. You will use a real research method that contemporary technical communicators and UX professionals use, and you will apply the course's 22 principles as a domain-specific heuristic set. The goal is to produce findings that are grounded in evidence, organized for a reader, and tied to concrete recommendations, the kind of deliverable a workplace would actually use.

### Where to start

First, choose your method:

- **Heuristic evaluation:** You (as expert evaluator) inspect a system against a defined set of heuristics, flag where it honors or violates each, and rate the severity of problems. You will adapt a heuristic set, combining or selecting from Nielsen's 10 usability heuristics and the course's 22 principles, and justify your adaptation.
- **Usability study:** You recruit 2 to 3 participants, give them realistic tasks on the system, observe them (using think-aloud), and document where they succeed, struggle, or fail. You may also collect a standardized measure such as the System Usability Scale (SUS).

Then choose your system. Strong candidates are interfaces rich enough to surface real issues: a game's onboarding or HUD, a self-checkout kiosk, a patient portal, tax or banking software, an EV charging interface, or assembly instructions. Confirm you can access the system enough to evaluate it thoroughly.

### What is due

A structured findings report, posted to the discussion forum. The report should include:

- A brief description of the system and why you chose it.
- Your method, and (for heuristic evaluation) your adapted heuristic set with justification, or (for usability study) your participant profile, tasks, and procedure.
- Your findings, organized clearly (by principle, by severity, or by task), with specific evidence for each.
- Concrete, prioritized recommendations tied to your findings.

Include two or three specific questions for peer feedback and indicate which areas you are open to opinions on.

### Tips and resources

- Organize findings so a busy reader can act. Severity ratings and clear headings matter as much as the findings themselves.
- Tie every claim to evidence. "The onboarding violates Principle 11 (chunk information)" needs a specific example showing where and how.
- For usability studies, keep tasks realistic and write them in plain language. Pilot them on a friend first.
- For think-aloud, your job is to listen and observe, not to coach. Let participants struggle; the struggle is the data.
- Required reading supports this directly: Nielsen on heuristic evaluation, Hayhoe & Brewer Ch. 5 (qualitative method), Ch. 7 (usability studies), and Ch. 12 (a worked usability report exemplar).
- The 22 principles in Appendix B are your domain-specific heuristic set. Use them.

## Major Assignment 3: Original Analysis Project

**Due: Week 15 (Sunday, December 6)**

### Assignment rationale

The culminating work of the course asks you to make an original argument: that a nontraditional site demonstrates and advances one or more technical communication principles, in conversation with the scholarship we have read. This is where you move from applying methods to contributing an idea. Strong projects do not merely show that a principle is present (Assignment 1) or evaluate a system against criteria (Assignment 2); they argue that the site teaches the field something, extending or complicating how technical communicators understand a principle. This is the analytical move at the heart of the course: games and interactive media as innovators, not just illustrations.

### Where to start

Choose a nontraditional site and one or more principles you want to argue about. The strongest projects often start from a tension or a surprise: a site that operationalizes a principle in an unexpected way, that advances a principle beyond how the field typically applies it, or that reveals the limits of a principle. Ground your argument in at least three course readings, putting your analysis in conversation with existing scholarship. Decide early whether a conventional seminar paper or a nonconventional form best serves your argument, and if nonconventional, get instructor approval.

### What is due

An original analysis project, posted to the discussion forum, in one of these forms:

- A seminar-style analysis paper (roughly 2,500 to 3,500 words) with APA citations.
- A nonconventional form with instructor approval: a video essay, an interactive Twine analysis, a designed annotated playthrough, or a podcast-style audio analysis, accompanied by a short written rationale (roughly 500 words) explaining your argument and citing your sources.

Whatever the form, the project must make an argument (not just a description), engage at least three course readings, and use evidence from the site to support its claims. Include your Critical Response questions and your openness indicators.

### Tips and resources

- Argue, do not summarize. A reader should be able to state your claim in one sentence and see why it is not obvious.
- "Demonstrates and advances" is the key phrase. Pushing past "this game shows Principle X" toward "this game advances Principle X by doing Y, which the field has not fully accounted for" is what distinguishes strong projects.
- Consider a counter-example or limitation. Acknowledging where the site fails or where the principle breaks down strengthens, rather than weakens, your argument.
- You are encouraged to submit this work to the appropriate graduate showcase or for conference consideration. Build it as something you could develop further.
- Use the manuscript chapters as models for the "demonstrates and advances" argumentative pattern.
- If choosing a nonconventional form, let the form serve the argument, not the novelty. A video essay should do something a paper could not.
