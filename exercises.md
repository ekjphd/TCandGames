Throughout this term, you will keep a notebook (or dev.log, an abbreviation for a "game developer's log"), on a Google doc, a Word doc, or even on paper (uploading legible images for me to grade). You will add to this each week when there is not a major assignment due. The point is to keep your eye sharp and your examples fresh, and to build a library of technical communication operating in the wild.

**Game Rules:**

- Each week, your exercise should connect to one of the principles or key topics from that week's readings. Most options work with any principle; pick one that fits.
- You may use any single option a maximum of **two times** across the term. Play around and experiment!
- Wherever you can, choose sites outside the AAA, "best games" mainstream: indie, mobile, older, non-Western, or non-game interfaces. The website [itch.io](https://itch.io/) houses many free indie games that are far different from the commercial blockbusters that typically come to mind when thinking about "video games."
- Many of these favor non-examples of principles. A broken interface often teaches a principle more clearly than a polished one.
- Keep posts focused: a few hundred words plus media, unless an option says otherwise.

### A. The Hall of Shame

Find a non-example: a site that violates the week's principle. Show exactly where it breaks down and what the failure costs the user. Bonus points for a failure so instructive it could be a textbook warning.

### B. Sound-Off / Sight-Off

Experience a game segment with the sound muted, then again with the visuals obscured (look away, listen only). Document what each modality alone communicates, and what collapses when you remove the other.

### C. Onboarding Teardown

Take the first five minutes of a game or app and map it move by move against the week's principle. Where does it teach you something? Where does it assume you already know?

### D. The Newbie Test

Hand a game or interface to someone who has never used it (a roommate, a family member, a friend) for a few minutes. Watch quietly. Report where they thrived, where they floundered, and what the design did or failed to do to help.

### E. Jargon Audit

Catalog the specialized terms a site teaches its users. How does it onboard you into its private language, or does it just assume you'll figure it out? Analyze one term's introduction in detail.

### F. Haptic Field Notes

Spend a day noticing the embodied and tactile feedback in the devices you already use: phone, car, watch, appliance. Catalog the cues and analyze what each one communicates without a single word.

### G. Genre Expectations Swap

Pick a game genre you have never played. Predict its conventions before you touch it. Then play or watch and analyze the gap between what you expected and what you found.

### H. The Time Machine

Find an older example (pre-2010, ideally pre-2000) of the week's principle. Show how the principle was operating before the technology we take for granted existed. What has changed, and what has not?

### I. Localization Detective

Find a site that was translated or adapted across languages or regions. Examine how the technical communication shifted, or failed to shift, and what got lost or gained in the move.

### J. The Accessibility Pass

Evaluate a site for one specific accessibility dimension (colorblind support, captions, one-handed play, screen-reader compatibility). Where does it include, and where does it exclude?

### K. Two Games Enter

Put two sites side by side that handle the same principle differently. Argue which does it better and why, with evidence from both. No fence-sitting.

### L. The Tutorial That Wasn't

Find a game that teaches a mechanic without any explicit instruction, purely through level design, environment, or consequence. Reverse-engineer how it taught you.

### M. Patch Notes

Find a case where a site changed its design over time (an update, a redesign, a sequel). Analyze what the change reveals about the principle, and whether players or users revolted.

### N. The Mundane Marvel

Find the week's principle operating in the most boring interface you can: a parking meter, a microwave, a tax form, an elevator panel. Make us see why it is secretly fascinating.

### O. Steal This Idea

Identify something a game does well with the week's principle and design a one-paragraph proposal for how a real-world technical communication product (a manual, a help system, a device) could borrow it.

### P. The Cognitive Load Confession

Find a site that overwhelms you. Document the moment your brain hit capacity, diagnose which signals competed for attention, and propose what the design should have cut or chunked.

### Q. Speedrun the Manual

Find official documentation for a game or product (a wiki, a help page, an in-game codex). Time how long it takes to find one specific answer. Analyze what helped or hindered the search.

### R. The Embodiment Experiment

Find a site that uses physical input or movement (motion controls, VR, AR, haptics, a wearable). Analyze how the body becomes part of the communication, and what that adds or costs.

## How to post

For each exercise, your discussion post should:

- Name the option letter and the principle you are connecting to.
- Identify the site clearly (with a link or footage reference where relevant).
- Show your evidence (screenshots, clips, or detailed description), not just describe it.
- Land one clear analytical point. What does this example teach us about the principle?

Then respond to at least two peers' posts with substantive replies.

## What counts as a substantive reply

A substantive reply extends the conversation rather than closing it. "Great example, I agree!" is not substantive. A reply that makes your peer think, see their example differently, or want to respond back is. Aim for replies that do at least one of the following:

- **Build on the analysis.** Take your peer's observation somewhere new: connect it to another principle, another site, or a course reading they did not mention.
- **Offer a counter-case.** Name a site that handles the same principle differently, or a moment where the principle your peer found actually breaks down.
- **Deepen the evidence.** Point to a specific detail in their example they did not fully unpack, and explain what it adds.
- **Bridge to the field.** Connect their game or interface observation to a real-world technical communication problem or product.

## Using the Critical Response Process in replies

This course uses an adaptation of Liz Lerman's Critical Response Process (CRP) for peer feedback. The full four-step process governs feedback on the major assignments, but its habits should shape your weekly replies too. Two CRP moves in particular make weekly replies stronger and more respectful:

**Lead with statements of meaning.** Before any suggestion, name something specific in your peer's post that was effective, surprising, or clarifying, and say what made it land. Be specific: "the muted-audio comparison made me realize the health bar was doing more work than I thought" is a statement of meaning; "nice job" is not.

**Ask neutral questions, not opinions in disguise.** A neutral question genuinely opens inquiry and does not steer your peer toward what you would have done. "What led you to read that chime as a warning rather than a confirmation?" is neutral. "Why didn't you consider that it might be a confirmation?" is an opinion wearing a question's clothing. Neutral questions invite your peer to articulate their reasoning, which often advances the analysis more than a flat disagreement would.

If you want to offer an opinion or a redirect, you may, but frame it as your reading rather than the correct one, and tie it to evidence. The goal of CRP is feedback that serves the creator's thinking, not feedback that redirects their work toward yours.
