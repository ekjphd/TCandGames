## Course Description

In this asynchronous, online course, students analyze video games as complex, interactive communication systems that operationalize and advance core principles of technical communication. The course focuses on how games structure instruction, feedback, and user guidance through multimodal interaction, including text, sound, visuals, and embodied input. Students apply technical communication research methods such as usability testing, heuristic evaluation, and user studies to evaluate game design and player experience. These analyses position video games as instructive models for contemporary technical communication challenges, including interactive documentation, AI-mediated communication, and accessibility-driven interface design.

This course treats games as one example within a broader category of nontraditional technical communication. Throughout the term, students will learn to recognize technical communication principles operating in games, interactive media, and other interfaces that fall outside conventional manuals and documents, and to argue for what these sites can teach the field.

---

## Student Learning Outcomes

After successful completion of this course, students will be able to:

- Identify and analyze technical communication principles operating in nontraditional sites such as video games and interactive media.
- Apply technical communication research methods (heuristic evaluation, usability testing, user studies) to interactive systems.
- Evaluate how multimodal design (text, visual, aural, haptic) conveys instruction and feedback under real-world constraints.
- Connect game design practices to contemporary technical communication problems, including AI-mediated communication and accessibility.
- Produce and share original analysis and research that contributes to scholarly conversation in technical communication and game studies.
- Provide structured, constructive peer feedback using a critical response framework.

## Major Assignments

This course has three major assignments, each shared in a discussion forum and responded to by peers using the modified Critical Response Process (described below). The three build toward each other: the first sharpens observation, the second applies a research method, and the third extends analysis into an original contribution.

### Major Assignment 1: Principle in the Wild (due Week 5)
Students identify a single technical communication principle operating in a nontraditional site of their choosing (a game, app, kiosk, wearable, vehicle interface, or other interactive system), and produce a short multimodal analysis (a narrated screen capture, annotated image set, or illustrated written piece) showing the principle in action. The deliverable emphasizes close observation and naming what is happening, not yet evaluating it. Shared in discussion; peers respond using the Critical Response Process.

### Major Assignment 2: Heuristic Evaluation or Usability Study (due Week 10)
Students select an interactive system and conduct either a heuristic evaluation (applying an established or adapted heuristic set) or a small usability study (2 to 3 participants, observed completing tasks). The deliverable is a structured findings report with evidence and recommendations. Students adapt a technical communication or game usability heuristic framework and justify their adaptation. Shared in discussion; peers respond using the Critical Response Process.

### Major Assignment 3: Original Analysis Project (due Week 15)
Students produce an original analysis arguing how a nontraditional site demonstrates and advances one or more technical communication principles, in conversation with course readings. The project may take a conventional seminar-paper form or a nonconventional form (a video essay, an interactive Twine analysis, a designed annotated playthrough, a podcast-style audio analysis), with instructor approval. Students are encouraged to submit this work to the appropriate graduate showcase or for conference consideration. Shared in discussion; peers respond using the Critical Response Process.

## Weekly Exercises and Discussions

Most weeks include a smaller exercise designed to build observation and analysis skills and to keep students engaged with one another's thinking. These are lower-stakes and often nonconventional. Examples:

- **Sound-off analysis:** Play or watch a game segment with the sound muted, then again with visuals obscured, and document what each modality alone communicates.
- **Onboarding teardown:** Map the first five minutes of a game's onboarding against a technical communication principle set.
- **Jargon audit:** Identify specialized terms a game teaches and analyze how (or whether) it onboards the player into them.
- **Failure log:** Document a moment where an interface (game or otherwise) failed to communicate, and diagnose the breakdown.
- **Haptic field notes:** Catalog the embodied and tactile feedback in a device you use daily and analyze what each cue communicates.
- **Genre expectations swap:** Predict the conventions of an unfamiliar game genre, then test your predictions and analyze the gap.

## The Critical Response Process (Modified for Asynchronous Online)

This course uses an adaptation of Liz Lerman's Critical Response Process for peer feedback on major assignments. The process is structured to produce useful, respectful, and specific feedback. When responding to a peer's shared major assignment, post your response in the following four-step order:

**Step 1: Statements of meaning.** Begin by noting what was meaningful, striking, or effective in the work. Be specific about what prompted the response.

**Step 2: Creator's questions (asynchronous adaptation).** Each creator includes, with their posted assignment, two or three specific questions they want feedback on. Responders address these questions before raising their own.

**Step 3: Neutral questions from responders.** Ask genuinely neutral questions about the creator's choices (not opinions disguised as questions). "What led you to organize the findings by severity?" is neutral; "Why didn't you organize by severity?" is not.

**Step 4: Opinions, with permission.** Offer an opinion only by naming the subject and asking permission first, in writing: "I have an opinion about the heuristic set you chose, would you like to hear it?" Because this is asynchronous, the creator signals openness in advance by checking a box or noting in their post which areas they are open to receiving opinions on.

Peer responses that skip steps or lead with unsolicited opinions do not meet the assignment requirements. The goal is feedback that serves the creator's intentions, not feedback that redirects the work toward what the responder would have done.
